// PROJECT 02 — CASE STUDY

Charger: EV Charging Cable Alert System

A Raspberry Pi Zero 2W dashboard companion that watches my car's charging indicator light with a camera and alerts me before I forget to plug in.

In Progress — Design & Architecture Phase
Raspberry Pi Zero 2W Python + OpenCV Computer Vision Embedded Hardware Portfolio Build
01

Overview

The problem is small but annoying: it's easy to walk away from an EV without actually getting the cable seated. Charger solves it with a tiny, always-on watcher.

A USB camera mounted near the dashboard stays pointed at the car's charging status light. A Raspberry Pi Zero 2W samples that light every second or so using OpenCV, checking whether it's on or off. If the light indicates the cable has disconnected — or was never connected — the system sounds a GPIO-driven buzzer and fires a push notification to my phone, before I've walked away from the car.

It's a self-contained unit: camera, compute, and power all live in one 3D-printed enclosure that sits on the dash, held down with suction cups and powered by a battery bank charged from the car's USB port. There's no screen and no speaker — just a light sensor, a buzzer, and a phone notification, kept as simple as the problem allows.

05

How Detection Works

The loop is deliberately simple — the Zero 2W has limited headroom, so the detection logic favors cheap, periodic checks over continuous video processing.

  1. 1

    Capture

    Grab a single frame from the USB camera roughly once a second.

  2. 2

    Crop to region of interest

    Isolate a small, calibrated pixel window covering the dashboard's charging indicator — ignore the rest of the frame entirely.

  3. 3

    Threshold in HSV

    Convert the crop to HSV color space and count pixels within a tuned red-hue range. HSV holds up far better than raw RGB against changing sunlight and dashboard glare, since hue stays comparatively stable even when brightness swings.

  4. 4

    Decide connected vs. disconnected

    Compare the red-pixel count against a calibrated threshold to flip the connection state.

  5. 5

    Alert on change

    On a confirmed state change, fire a push notification through ntfy.sh and drive the buzzer — two beeps for "connected," one for "disconnected" — then keep issuing a periodic reminder beep for as long as the cable stays connected.

  6. 6

    Sleep

    Wait roughly a second before the next poll — there's no need to check faster than the light itself changes.

06

Next Iteration — Debounce Refinement

The live script (above) already works, but it re-evaluates on every single poll, which makes it sensitive to a stray glare frame or a blinking indicator. The next revision I'm planning adds a confirm-streak: the "off" reading has to persist across several consecutive polls before it triggers an alert. Sketch below — not yet merged into the live script.

detector_v2.py — planned debounce loop (sketch)
ROI = (140, 95, 160, 115)  # x1, y1, x2, y2 — calibrated once during setup
BRIGHTNESS_THRESHOLD = 80
POLL_INTERVAL_SEC = 4
CONFIRM_COUNT = 3

off_streak = 0

while True:
    ok, frame = cap.read()
    if not ok:
        time.sleep(POLL_INTERVAL_SEC)
        continue

    gray = cv2.cvtColor(frame, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY)
    x1, y1, x2, y2 = ROI
    region = gray[y1:y2, x1:x2]
    brightness = region.mean()

    if brightness < BRIGHTNESS_THRESHOLD:
        off_streak += 1
    else:
        off_streak = 0

    if off_streak >= CONFIRM_COUNT:
        notify("Charging cable is not connected.")
        beep(1)
        off_streak = 0  # avoid re-triggering every loop

    time.sleep(POLL_INTERVAL_SEC)
07

Alerts & Enclosure

🔔

Alerts

A GPIO-driven piezo buzzer sounds two beeps on connect, one on disconnect, plus a periodic reminder beep while the cable stays connected. A push notification fires the instant the state changes — no screen, no local UI, status just lives on my phone.

📦

Enclosure

Multiple 3D-printed iterations to fit the camera and Pi Zero 2W without stacking them. The final shell houses both side-by-side in a flat, low-profile case with a screwed-on lid, mounted to the dash on suction-cup feet.

08

Design Challenges

Lighting variability

Sunlight, headlight glare, and time-of-day changes affect how red pixels read in-frame. Calibration — and eventually the debounce logic above — is needed to avoid false alarms, not just a fixed brightness threshold.

Camera-to-enclosure fit

The USB camera turned out to be physically larger than the Pi Zero 2W it was supposed to sit on top of, which broke the original stacked-enclosure plan and forced a full redesign around a side-by-side layout.

Power system safety

A battery "HAT" seemed like the obvious power solution until I researched lithium battery behavior under heat — a car dashboard in direct sun ruled it out entirely, and the design moved to an external battery bank kept in a shaded spot.

Compute headroom on the Zero 2W

512MB of RAM and a modest quad-core chip mean continuous video processing isn't realistic — the periodic polling approach exists specifically to stay within budget.

The very first printed shell, sized around the Raspberry Pi Zero 2W alone — before I'd found out the camera wasn't going to fit above it the way I'd planned. Two small cutouts test port alignment for power and data.

A slightly resized shell testing exact cutout placement, so the Pi's connectors line up without needing oversized holes that would weaken the print.

Iter. 03

Widening the window

Cutouts expanded to test a larger window alongside the original port hole — an early attempt to make room for the camera without a full redesign, before I accepted the stacked layout just wasn't going to work.

Iter. 04

Standalone camera housing

A dedicated, taller housing built after realizing the camera was physically bigger than the Pi it was supposed to sit on top of. This is also around when I was testing a lithium battery "HAT" for power, before ruling it out on heat-safety grounds and switching to an external battery bank.

Iter. 05

Merged, vertical attempt

An attempt to merge the camera and Pi back into one enclosure, with a round lens cutout on the front face. It worked, but the vertical stack was bulkier than I wanted sitting on a dashboard.

Iter. 06

The winning geometry

A flat, low-profile shell with the camera up front behind an oval lens window and the Pi tucked in back — no more vertical stacking. This shape is what shipped.

Final

Assembled & mounted

The finished, assembled unit — screwed-shut lid, camera and GPIO wiring routed internally, and four suction-cup feet mounted on threaded posts. This is the version that sits on the dash today, positioned directly in front of the car's charging indicator.

11

Why This Project

Charger is a compact demonstration of end-to-end embedded systems work: reading a real-world analog signal — a light — through a camera, processing it under tight compute constraints, and turning that into a clear, human-facing alert, all wrapped in a purpose-built physical enclosure that went through real, documented iteration. It touches computer vision, mechanical and enclosure design, hardware integration, and power engineering in one small package.
